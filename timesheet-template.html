Why paper timesheets leak money

Hours scribbled on paper get added up at week's end — from memory, in a hurry, often by someone who wasn't on site. The result is the same on most crews: rounded-up hours, missed overtime flags, and no way to tell which job the hours actually belong to. Payroll gets paid either way; it's the job costing that quietly goes wrong.

How to keep time records that hold up

  • Record hours the day they're worked. Friday-night reconstruction is where overtime gets missed and hours get rounded. Same rule as safety paperwork: capture it as you go.
  • Split regular time and overtime as you write. If OT lives in a separate column from day one, payroll disputes and remittance errors mostly disappear.
  • Always tie hours to a job or cost code. Payroll only needs totals — but your estimates need to know which job ate the hours. That column is what makes next year's quotes sharper.
  • Get a signature. A signed sheet settles "I worked more than that" conversations before they start, and it's the record an auditor or WCB claim will ask for.
  • Keep the sheets somewhere the office can pull them. A photo of a muddy clipboard is not a filing system.

Can I track multiple jobs on one sheet? This sheet covers one job/site per page — cleanest for job costing. If your crew bounces between jobs daily, that's exactly where paper breaks down and an app that logs time per job earns its keep.
